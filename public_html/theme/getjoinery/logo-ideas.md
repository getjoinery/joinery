# Logo Ideas: Japanese Joinery Influence

The word "Joinery" already does most of the work. The goal is to reinforce the
metaphor visually without making it literal (no cartoon wood joints, no
hammers, no Japanese calligraphy).

## What Japanese joinery actually communicates

- Pieces that fit together without external fasteners (no nails = no duct tape)
- Structural integrity through design, not brute force
- Visible craftsmanship — the joint itself is beautiful
- Precision and intentionality
- Longevity — these joints last centuries

All of these map directly to what Joinery (the software) stands for.


## Ideas: Typographic treatments

### 1. The split letterform
Take one letter in "Joinery" (the J, the i, or the y) and split it into two
pieces that interlock. The letter still reads correctly, but on close inspection
you see two separate shapes fitting together like a joint. Subtle — most people
won't notice until they look twice.

**Best candidate:** The J — its hook and stem can be split into two interlocking
pieces, like a simple mortise and tenon viewed from the side.

### 2. The notched crossbar
The word "Joinery" in Manrope (current heading font). The crossbar of the "e"
or the dot of the "i" is replaced with a tiny interlocking notch — two shapes
that fit together. Like a finger joint at tiny scale. Almost invisible at
nav size, noticeable at hero size.

### 3. Ligature joint
Where two letters meet (the "in" pair, or "er" pair), the letterforms overlap
with a small dovetail-style interlock instead of just butting up against each
other. The letters literally join.


## Ideas: Logomark (icon next to or above the wordmark)

### 4. Two interlocking L-shapes
Two simple geometric L-shapes (or bracket shapes) that slot together to form a
square or rectangle. Minimal, geometric, works at favicon size. Think of a
Japanese "kama-tsugi" (sickle joint) simplified to pure geometry:

```
  ┌──┐
  │  └──┐
  │     │
┌─┘  ┌──┘
│    │
└────┘
```

This is essentially two pieces that can only come apart by sliding — not by
pulling. That's the Japanese joinery principle: the geometry itself holds.

### 5. The dovetail mark
A single dovetail shape — a trapezoid wider at the top than the bottom. One of
the most recognizable joint profiles. Used alone as a simple mark, it reads as
"craftsmanship" and "precision fit" without needing explanation. Works well as
a favicon or app icon.

```
   ╱────╲
  ╱      ╲
 ╱        ╲
 ──────────
```

### 6. Negative space joint
A square with a clean gap running through it — but the gap isn't straight, it
follows a simple step pattern (like a lap joint). The two halves are clearly
separate pieces that fit together perfectly. The gap is the logo.

```
 ┌────┬──┐
 │    │  │
 │  ┌─┘  │
 │  │    │
 └──┴────┘
```


## Ideas: Subtle accents (lowest touch)

### 7. The corner detail
Keep the current text-only "Joinery" logo. Add a tiny interlocking corner
detail — two small L-shapes that slot together — in one corner of the nav logo
area or as a decorative element. Like a craftsman's mark.

### 8. Favicon only
The wordmark stays clean text. The favicon/app icon is where the joinery
reference lives — a simple interlocking geometric shape that works at 16x16.
This keeps the site clean while giving the brand mark depth.


## Recommendations

**For immediate use:** Option 4 (two interlocking L-shapes) as a small mark
before the wordmark, plus Option 8 (same shape as favicon). It's geometric
enough to feel like a tech logo, but the interlocking principle is unmistakably
joinery.

**For a future designer:** Option 1 (split J letterform) is the most
sophisticated approach but needs a real type designer to execute well.

**What to avoid:**
- Literal wood grain textures
- Japanese aesthetic pastiche (torii gates, cherry blossoms, etc.)
- Anything that requires explanation ("see, the two pieces fit together like...")
- Overly complex marks that don't work at small sizes
